When a human forensic examiner collects evidence, the first thing they do is
seal it. Hash the disk image, log who touched it and when, and never work on
the original. Nobody extends this courtesy to AI agents — we let them read
production state, mutate it mid-investigation, and then we wonder whether the
timeline in their report describes the incident or describes what the agent
did to the incident.

opsbench applies **NIST SP 800-86 discipline to agent investigations**,
and it changes what the output is worth.

## Quarantine before anything

The first act of an investigation is to stop the bleeding *and* stop the
trampling: clients scaled to zero, a default-deny network policy around the
affected workloads. Not only containment — evidence preservation. An
investigation that runs while traffic keeps rewriting the state is studying a
moving target.

## Seal at collection time

Collectors sweep seven layers in parallel — application, node, network,
storage, control plane, observability, history. Everything they capture is
sealed immediately:

```text
sha256:9f2a11c4…  node-collector   journal-worker-3.log     SEALED
sha256:4be80d21…  storage-collector pvc-metrics-0.snapshot  SEALED
```

A cataloger indexes the evidence; a separate witness agent attests the
manifest. From that point, every claim in every report must cite a sealed
artifact by `file:timestamp`. If an agent asserts something it cannot pin to
the manifest, the citation checker blocks the artifact.

## Verdict-blind investigation

The subtle failure mode of agent investigations is consensus by contamination:
one confident early hypothesis leaks into every later prompt, and the
"independent" analyses converge on it. opsbench runs hypothesis agents
**verdict-blind** — one subagent per hypothesis, none aware of the others'
conclusions, each grading only its own theory against the sealed evidence.
The forensic synthesizer is the first thing allowed to see all verdicts at
once — the same reason the NTSB runs party submissions before the board writes
the final report.

## Why bother

Because the alternative is trusting a narrative. A sealed-evidence
investigation can be *re-run*: hand the manifest to a different model, a
different team, or an auditor, and the citations either hold or they do not.
That property — reconstructability — is what separates a report you can act
on from a story that sounds right.

Evidence, or it didn't happen.
